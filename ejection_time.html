Ejection Time Testing

Requirement Overview

Definition: The time required for the complete ejection of the drug solution from the auto-injector must be comparable to the RLD.

Regulatory Basis: This is a supportive comparative characterization study introduced in the 2018 guidance and maintained in subsequent guidance documents.

Critical Quality Attribute: Ejection time affects drug delivery completeness, patient experience, and potentially drug distribution in tissue.

Test Method and Equipment

Required Equipment:

  • High-speed camera (minimum 240 fps, preferably 1000+ fps)
  • Transparent collection vessel with calibrated markings
  • Controlled lighting system
  • Video analysis software with frame-by-frame capability
  • Calibrated timer or frame counter
  • Temperature-controlled environment

Test Procedure

  1. Condition all test units at controlled temperature (20-25°C) for at least 24 hours
  2. Set up the high-speed camera perpendicular to the injection path
  3. Ensure proper lighting to clearly visualize the needle and ejected solution
  4. Position the auto-injector in the test fixture with the needle pointing into the transparent collection vessel
  5. Start the high-speed camera recording
  6. Activate the auto-injector according to the instructions for use
  7. Continue recording until the ejection is visibly complete
  8. Using the video analysis software, determine the ejection start time (first appearance of liquid at needle tip) and end time (last visible drop or cessation of flow)
  9. Calculate the ejection time as the difference between end and start times
  10. Repeat for all test units across all batches
  11. Perform identical testing on the reference product units

Data Analysis

Statistical Analysis:

While PBE analysis is not specifically required for ejection time, a comprehensive statistical comparison should include:

  1. Calculate means and standard deviations for test and reference products
  2. Perform appropriate statistical tests to compare distributions (e.g., t-test, ANOVA)
  3. Evaluate batch-to-batch variability
  4. Assess whether the test product ejection time falls within the range established by the RLD
Critical Consideration: Ejection time that is too fast may cause patient discomfort or affect drug distribution in tissue, while ejection time that is too slow may result in incomplete delivery if the patient removes the device prematurely.
