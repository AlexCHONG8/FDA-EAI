Trigger Force Testing

Requirement Overview

Definition: The force required to activate the auto-injector must be comparable to the RLD, ensuring usability while preventing accidental activation.

Regulatory Basis: This is a supportive comparative characterization study introduced in the 2018 guidance and maintained in subsequent guidance documents.

Critical Quality Attribute: Trigger force affects usability, accessibility for patients with limited hand strength, and protection against accidental activation.

Test Method and Equipment

Required Equipment:

  • Force gauge with appropriate range (typically 5-50N) and precision
  • Mechanical test stand with controlled rate of force application
  • Fixture to hold auto-injector in the correct orientation
  • Data acquisition system to record force profiles
  • Calibration weights or reference standards

Test Procedure

  1. Condition all test units at controlled temperature (20-25°C) and humidity (40-60% RH) for at least 24 hours
  2. Calibrate the force gauge using certified standards
  3. Position the auto-injector in the test fixture, ensuring the activation mechanism is properly aligned with the force gauge
  4. Configure the test stand to apply force at the specified rate
  5. Start the data acquisition system to record the force profile
  6. Apply force until the auto-injector activates
  7. Record the peak force required for activation
  8. Reset the test fixture and prepare for the next unit
  9. Repeat for all test units across all batches

Data Analysis

Statistical Analysis:

While PBE analysis is not specifically required for trigger force, a comprehensive statistical comparison should include:

  1. Calculate means and standard deviations for test and reference products
  2. Perform appropriate statistical tests to compare distributions (e.g., t-test, ANOVA)
  3. Evaluate batch-to-batch variability
  4. Assess whether the test product trigger force falls within the range established by the RLD
Critical Consideration: Trigger force must balance two competing requirements: it must be low enough to be usable by the intended patient population (including those with limited hand strength) but high enough to prevent accidental activation during carrying or handling.
